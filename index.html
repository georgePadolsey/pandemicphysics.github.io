However, across all mea-sures, the UK excess death rate from COVID-19 has beenone of the worst worldwide [1]. A number of areas have been of particular interest interms of academic research related to the pandemic. Asof 2021, vaccines are by far the most prominent but thereare many others. For instance, the medical physics aspectof the scientific response is of particular interest. Medicalphysics is simply the term used to describe physics that canbe used to treat, diagnose and gain understanding of issueswithin the field of medicine. Radiation treatment for cancerand X-ray imaging are particularly well known examplesbut medical physics has also proven highly useful in com-bating COVID-19. Here, certain areas of relevant medicalphysics are explained and the success of their implementa-tions analysed.

Our statistical models of the spread of COVID-19 haveguided governments in their approach to COVID-19 mea-sures and UK lockdown. An understanding of the now wellknown ’R number’ requires an appreciation of the exponen-tial function and the mathematics of disease spread. Thegeneral public’s understanding of COVID-19 is strongly in-fluenced by government briefings, which make heavy use ofstatistical data. Data presentation and scientific communi-cation are key tools for any government attempting to per-suade the public of the necessity of COVID-19 measures:If not presented accurately and consistently this, alongsidemisinformation, can shape trust between the public and thegovernment in dangerous ways.

Our understanding of common physical processes hasoverseen successful mass testing for COVID-19. LateralFlow Tests can only be understood with a knowledge ofcapillary flow and surface tension, whilst other diagnosticmethods use far more sensitive enzyme reactions. Onlywhen we know the details of how each test works can wedecide which is appropriate for this pandemic- mass testingis clearly required but how far can we sacrifice sensitivity?The future of disease testing, not only for COVID-19, is ofparticular interest- with CT scanning and use of neural net-works as particularly fast moving research topics.

A consideration of how droplet motion governs the de-sign of masks seems particularly relevant with the knowl-edge that COVID-19 predominantly spreads through the air[2]. Additionally, with a multitude of masks available tothe public, me must also understand why different types ofmasks are more effective than others. A surprising rangeof common physical concepts help explain droplet motion-from intuitive Newtonian mechanics, to electromagnetism,to statistical models of random motion common in thermo-dynamics.
